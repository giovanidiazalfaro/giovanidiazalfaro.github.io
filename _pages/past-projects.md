### <em>Externalized and Extended Cognition</em>: Cognitive Offloading for Human-Machine Teaming

The goal of this project was to provide recommendations for mitigating workload in human-machine teaming contexts (e.g. AI as teammate), as well as offer a multi-disciplinarily informed set of theoretically grounded research questions and designs for understanding and improving workload in manned-unmanned teaming environments. 

* <strong>Role:</strong> Research Lead
* <strong>Sponsor:</strong> Lockheed Martin Corporation (PI: Stephen M. Fiore)
* <strong>Activity Period:</strong> Spring 2023 -  November 2024

<strong>The problem as it was handed to me:</strong> Lockheed Martin and the U.S. Army needed to understand how to reduce pilot cognitive workload in next-generation manned-unmanned aviation systems (Future Vertical Lift). They had a detailed cognitive task analysis of the operational environment, but no clear theoretical bridge connecting what pilots actually need cognitively to concrete, testable design questions engineers could act on.

<strong>The choice I made, and why:</strong> I could have approached this purely from a human factors engineering angle — treating workload as something to measure and minimize. Instead, I applied a cognitive science framework (4E cognition: cognition as extended, embedded, enactive, embodied) because it let me ask a more useful question: not just "how much workload is there," but "where does cognitive processing actually live — in the pilot, the system, or the interaction between them?" That distinction pointed to two separable mechanisms — offloading and scaffolding — that engineers could design around independently, rather than treating workload as one undifferentiated problem.

<strong>What I produced:</strong> Three concrete research recommendations translating that framework into testable design questions — for example, that offloading techniques should be tested across varying levels of accessibility (required, optional, unavailable) to see how that choice itself affects cognitive efficiency under realistic task disruptions. I then designed experiments to test these recommendations directly.

<strong>What's still open:</strong> The hardest part of this work wasn't the theory — it was translating it into something a non-cognitive-scientist engineer could act on without oversimplifying it. If I were doing this again, I'd push earlier for direct engineer feedback on the recommendations themselves, before finalizing the experimental designs, to catch translation gaps sooner.

* <strong>Products:</strong>
  * <a href="https://doi.org/10.1177/10711813241275506"> Research Paper and Presentation at the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting (2024)</a>

### Cognition in Support of Future Space Missions

The goal of this project was to develop unobtrusive measures of team functioning for future space missions by leveraging multi-sensor data (e.g., physiological, environmental, etc.) to monitor individual and team cognitive states.

* <strong>Role:</strong> Research Staff, Experiment Coordinator 
* <strong>Sponsor:</strong> U.S. DoD Minerva Research Initiative (PI: Stephen M. Fiore)
* <strong>Activity period:</strong> June 2023 - Fall 2025
