<p class="has-text-align-center">What is it to be "existentially comfortable"?</p>

<p class="has-text-align-center">     To be existentially comfortable is to accept the persistent reality of existential discomfort.</p>

<p>Many might read that and think it to be a pointless answer -- I prefer to consider it <em>absurd</em>.  Existential discomfort is a byproduct of the realization (with or without acceptance) that there is no inherent source of meaning to be found in our lives, with an overwhelming sense of angst typically being the initial result of the former. </p>

<p>Along with this angst comes an unrivaled sense of freedom. A freedom impossible to acquire without this daunting and grim feat of intellectual exploration, and once acquired allows oneself to cultivate a sense of this elusive, paradoxical, existential comfort. </p>

<p>Existential thought seems to be a prime breeding ground for paradoxes. We seem prone to inquire about the meaning of life, meanwhile an answer is impossible. Albert Camus is an icon of the existential school of philosophical thought, but he denied being a philosopher. He also supports cultivation of the <em>absurd sensibility</em>, fueled by lucidity and conscious reflection -- yes, an <em>absurd sensibility</em>. </p>

<p>At this point, arguing for an existentially paradoxical sense of comfort only makes sense. Following realization of the existentialism truisms, one is typically overwhelmed with angst, this angst then transforming into a unique sense of freedom. Exercising this freedom liberally and intellectually allows for acknowledgement of the paradoxes, the impossible, and especially, the persistent discomfort that comes with these developments. Without this discomfort one can never strive to achieve existential comfort, and when achieved, choose to exist paradoxically themself. As Camus says, "One must imagine Sisyphus happy." </p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I leave Sisyphus at the foot of the mountain! One always finds one's burden again. But Sisyphus teaches the higher fidelity that negates the gods and raises rocks. He too concludes that all is well. This universe henceforth without a master seems to him neither sterile nor futile. Each atom of that stone, each mineral flake of that night filled mountain, in itself forms a world. The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy.</p>
<cite><em>The Myth of Sisyphus</em>, Albert Camus</cite></blockquote>
