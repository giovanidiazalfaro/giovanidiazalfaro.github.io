<blockquote>
<p style="text-align:center;">"If it is not right do not do it; if it is not true do not say it."</p>
<p style="text-align:center;">-Marcus Aurelius, <em>Meditations</em></p>
</blockquote>
As previously stated in my post "Core Values," I strive to hold myself to a degree of complete honesty. This has been, and continues to be, no easy task. Not because I enjoy lying, or because I dislike telling the truth, but because honesty doesn't fit as well into our modern context as most would think.

In pursuing this extreme degree of honesty, there are certain guidelines I have put in place for behaving and responding in all contexts.
<blockquote>
<ul>
	<li>If asked a question, answer honestly, or do not answer at all.</li>
	<li>If there is no need to speak, then don't.</li>
	<li>Hold yourself accountable.</li>
	<li>Always expect the worst possible outcome.</li>
	<li>Remind yourself that others are just as responsible for their own choices as you are those of your own.</li>
	<li>Convenience is not an excuse.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
These guidelines seem simple. They seem self-explanatory. But these guidelines, are deceptively difficult. Even when initially coming up with them, I considered them strictly from my own perspective. I would think to myself just how easy it will be to follow them all. But the moment I put them into practice, I realized just how wrong I was.

In terms of these guidelines, and pursuing honesty, carrying out the prescribed actions is initially the easy part. But once one starts having to deal with the effects of, and reactions to these actions, the true difficulty is realized. Not everyone is accustomed to such an extreme degree of honesty, let alone prepared to receive it. And above else, I wasn't prepared to properly carry it out. Next thing I knew, I was experiencing negative reactions from each and every direction I'd try to set out in honestly. People would ask me questions, I would answer honestly, and they would react with a mix of emotions. Anger, sadness, confusion, irritation, surprise, and dumbfoundedness were becoming a thousand times more common than happiness, calmness, and gratitude. And this just left me absolutely confused. Here I am, trying to hold myself to what I've decided is to be one of my core values, and no one seems to be benefitting.

So, I sat down and thought about it. If honesty is something so many people claim to desire, and something that is stressed to be of such moral and ethical importance in philosophy, then why is it doing no good in my case? And it was after asking that question, that I came to the conclusion that honesty doesn't yield benefit solely from being given. Honesty needs to be received as earnestly as it is given. I realized that not only was I not being smart in my usage of honesty and following of my guidelines, but also that others weren't prepared to receive the honesty they so eagerly desired. Here is where I learned to keep quiet when there is no need to speak, always expect the worst, and to remember that we are all responsible for our own choices.

Initially, I took a pretty pompous stance on my newly found sense of honesty. I would speak my mind unnecessarily, and unsurprisingly this had many more detrimental effects than beneficial ones. And so, I learned that silence and honesty could both be just as beneficial. Also, I learned that optimism and pessimism were both unnecessary. Be realistic, and always expect the worst. In this case, you can't be disappointed, you'll usually be prepared for anything, and anything beneficial will come as a positive surprise. And lastly, I learned to recognize the importance of the choices of others in the conversations I had experienced.

If someone makes a conscious choice to ask me a question, then they are making a choice to receive my answer, whether they are prepared for it or not. And the main issue I noticed here is that many were asking questions and expecting certain responses, meanwhile not preparing themselves for what they maybe weren't ready to hear.
<blockquote>
<p style="text-align:center;">"So, how are you liking it here?" -- "I'm not."</p>
<p style="text-align:center;">"Do you like me?" -- "No, not really."</p>
<p style="text-align:center;">"Why didn't you come?" -- "Because I didn't want to go."</p>
</blockquote>
These all seem like very simple, common questions and responses. But in reality, these are some of the most common scenarios in which I've been met with complete shock and contempt. I feel that an unnecessary phenomenon of social compliance and convenience has taken over interactions and relationships. In our modern society, it is almost as if we are supposed to have a duty to please others, or use the "white lie" to circumvent all negative reactions. Why is this? What true benefit arises from avoiding hearing a simple "no"? Although it might be more convenient to provide someone with the answer they desire, what justice have you done them by giving them an answer contrary to the truth? And above all else, what intrinsic justification makes it ok to soil your own character with such actions? If you have consciously decided that you prefer to please others than to be a person of honest character, than so be it, excel in your choice. But if one is to pursue virtue and a stoic temperament, then there is no room for such deception or lack of moral aptitude. Because at the end of the day, I am at peace knowing I have not deceived a single person today. I am at peace, knowing I gave each and every person not only an honest answer, but an honest effort, and honest behavior. Honesty doesn't simply mean answering questions truthfully, but also behaving in a virtuous manner that is in accordance with what you believe. If I'm going to strive to be an honest person, I can't commit actions I've proclaimed to be against my belief of what is good.

Regardless of the benefits of practicing honesty in pursuit of virtue, it's a tough path to take. The sheer power of the truth is something I am still struggling to come to terms with, and directing it correctly is proving to be just as difficult. Nonetheless, I will continue to strive to hold myself to a degree of complete honesty, and I will observe, and learn from the effects doing so has not only on myself, but others and the world around me. I do believe that honesty holds the potential for an immense benefit and good, but it takes effort on more than one part to yield such valuable results. And in terms of personal benefit, all that really matters is that I am holding myself to the values that I deem important. Because at the end of the day, I can lose my arms, my home, my job, or even be tossed in jail. But what can never be taken from me, and what is truly in my control, are my mind and my character.
