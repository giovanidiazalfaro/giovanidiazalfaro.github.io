<p>     At the moment I'm writing this, I've been running this blog for a whopping two whole days. And instead of trying to choose between the myriad topics for discussion I have flying around in my head, I've chosen to challenge myself and attempt to write a post on a subject unbeknownst to me. To do this, I've decided that I'm going to grab my copies of Seneca's <em>Letters from a Stoic</em>, and Marcus Aurelius' <em>Meditations</em>, close my eyes, pick a book, and open to up a random page. Then, I'm going to pick a random passage, and I'll go from there.</p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p>     "Say to yourself first thing in the morning: today I shall meet people who are meddling, ungrateful, aggressive, treacherous, malicious, unsocial. All this has afflicted them through their ignorance of true good and evil. But I have seen that the nature of good is what is right, and the nature of evil what is wrong; and I have reflected that the nature of the offender himself is akin to my own - not a kinship of blood or seed, but a sharing in the same mind, the same fragment of divinity. Therefore I cannot be harmed by any of them, as none will infect me with their wrong. Nor can I be angry with my kinsman or hate him. We were born for cooperation, like feet, like hands, like eyelids, like the rows of upper and lower teeth. So to work in opposition to one another is against nature: and anger or rejection is opposition." - Marcus Aurelius in <em>Meditations</em></p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p>     So, this is where I ended up -- the first paragraph of Book 2. One of the features I appreciate most regarding Marcus Aurelius and his work is his straightforwardness. He's blunt, direct, honest, and open. The phrase he suggests we say to ourselves exemplifies that greatly, because quite simply, it is true. Each and every day we come across "people who are meddling, ungrateful, aggressive, treacherous, malicious, unsocial," and more. And although we do tend to come across many who sit on the other ends of those spectrum, those are usually people whom we don't struggle to interact with. But almost every day we come across the angry morning driver swerving through traffic and honking his horn, flicking everyone off. Or the coworker who blatantly ignores all duties that require tending to, constantly getting by doing the bare minimum, and sometimes making everyone else's lives harder by doing so. Or, the innocently clueless neighbor, coworker, or pedestrian making everyone frustrated in some way, shape or form. These are all people who most don't go a day without encountering. Yet although this is the case, we still allow them to affect us mentally, emotionally, or physically (sometimes even several or all of those). In response to this, Marcus asks: Why? We as Stoics for example, have taken large amounts of time to develop ourselves, and ponder over ideas such as virtue, good, evil, etc. And because of that we have cultivated mindsets that drive our behaviors and thoughts on a day to day basis. So this being said, how ignorant would I have to be to expect everyone around me to act in accordance with these values, when I myself have to put effort into acting in accordance with them?</p>

<p>     As Marcus states, there is no difference between us and them. We share the same mind, and the same vessel; "the nature of the offender himself is akin to my own." In saying this he expresses the idea that we are all of the same base mold, and were all born to cooperate. If this is indeed the case, then what benefit lies in getting angry, frustrated, annoyed, or disappointed as a result of the actions of others. Instead of going to your coworker and wasting your own time talking about the insolence and lack of productivity harbored by a "lazy" coworker, why not either just work on improving your own performance, or take it a step further and try to address these issues with them. And I do not mean go tell them how bad they are at everything, and tell them they're lazy and dumb. I mean lend a helping hand. We haven't the slightest clue as to what is happening in the minds and lives of most people around us, or their motivations and reasons for acting the way they do. Being angry or discontent fosters an even greater detriment and discontinuity, ultimately benefiting no one; "to work in opposition to one another is against nature: and anger or rejection is opposition." The only way others can affect us, is if we choose to let them. That much, we are in control of. And in this case, we are left with two choices.</p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p style="text-align:center;">      <em>Practice Indifference, Acceptance, Tolerance, and Patience</em></p>

<p><em>     </em>These are all very important values in Stoic philosophy. And quite honestly, it would be ridiculous to expect anyone, let alone myself, to simply pick up and refine an ideal Stoic temperament. It is extremely difficult, and requires long arduous work, and great amounts of effort. Many, many mistakes will be made, and much progress will be made as well. But we can all start with realization. Our mindsets are entirely within our control. How things affect us, is entirely within our control. It is up to us whether or not we get annoyed by a traffic light only staying green long enough for four cars to make a left turn out of a line of twenty, or by someone else bumping into us and causing us to spill our coffee on ourselves. One key exercise I've adopted, is constantly telling myself, and believing, that it is what it is. What did getting frustrated truly accomplish? Oh wait -- nothing. Every night, I get home and half the time I have to park nowhere near my apartment because of a lack of parking. Does it suck? Sure does (especially when it's raining). But am I mad? Not the least bit. I work an evening job, and it's only natural that those who work during the day have secured the prime parking by the time I get home at one in the morning. Did my pants rip on my way to work getting into my car? They sure did. Do I need new pants before I go in? I sure do. Am I annoyed that I have to risk being late to work now to go spend money on a new pair of pants to even be able to go make money at work? Not the least bit. Why? Because it is what it is. Through patience and acceptance, one can cultivate a sense of tolerance--a sort of invulnerability to the many occurrences and effects of everyday life. And then once this tolerance has been developed, one can delve even deeper into the mind and self, and fortify the sense of indifference that results in the elimination of the difficulty and requiring of effort of such tolerance. The next time you're faced with a situation that fosters even the slightest negative feeling, just try to not only tell yourself, but believe, that it simply is what is is.</p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><em>Lend A Helping Hand </em></p>

<p>     Now the aforementioned choice, is an extremely important and difficult stepping stone on one of the many paths to cultivating a Stoic temperament. And once one has achieved a sense of indifference, even if only to a slight extent, they have allowed themselves a new opportunity. This opportunity, is to lend a helping hand. As stated by Marcus our kinsmen are just as human as we are. Some might be more seasoned in the Stoic practices than us, and some may be much more clueless to those practices than us. But nonetheless, what reason do we have to not help them progress from wherever they currently stand? Yes we ourselves may still have much progress to make, but we can learn just as much from trying to help others as they can from us trying to help them. It's not fair to simply expect everyone to be universally virtuous, and so on top of doing our part in trying to become so, we can try and help others do the same. Self-development is to me, a never-ending process. But once one has cultivated tools to assist in the process, I believe they are best used as frequently and universally as possible, both for oneself and others. Try and soothe the angry stranger who just had a car drive by and splash a puddle onto them. Try and suggest a better alternative to your struggling coworker. Show the aggressive and angry person that their actions have no effect on you whatsoever. Quite simply, do something that fosters learning and progress. The results more often than not, will not be immediate. And more often than not, you will not be met with positive reactions. But who cares? You know that you're doing what is good by nature, and practicing becoming a virtuous human being. And your actions, no matter how little or how large, when lending a helping hand, are having positive effects to some extent on the life of someone else, and your own. What more can we ask for than that?</p><p>&nbsp;</p>

<p>     "Today I shall meet people who are meddling, ungrateful, aggressive, treacherous, malicious, unsocial." Today, I will experience a wide variety of events, situations, and stimuli. Some will be good, some will be bad, and some neutral. But at the end of the day, I control not only how all these stimuli affect me, but also how I perceive them. I am in complete control over my interpretations and reactions. I have a choice to practice temperance and indifference. I have a choice to reach out. And each and every day, I have to make these choices second by second. Marcus Aurelius didn't simply become one of the most idealistically Stoic human beings to have ever existed by natural talent. He poured his being into practicing these virtues and ideas in everything he did. He strove to become a good, virtuous human being. And we too can strive to become so. Something as tiny as choosing to make one Stoic choice today can have a prodigious effect on not only your life, but also those around you. And I urge you to try to do so next time you face a situation that requires a virtue such as tolerance or patience. And if you feel so inclined, take it even further and attempt to lend a helping hand.</p>
