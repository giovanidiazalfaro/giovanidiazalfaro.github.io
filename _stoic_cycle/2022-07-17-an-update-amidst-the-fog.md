<h2>     preface: summary of <em>the</em> <em>myth of Sisyphus</em>, albert camus </h2>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>The Myth of Sisyphus, philosophical essay by Albert Camus, published in French in 1942 as Le Mythe de Sisyphe. Published in the same year as Camus’s novel L’Étranger (The Stranger), The Myth of Sisyphus contains a sympathetic analysis of contemporary nihilism and touches on the nature of the absurd. Together the two works established his reputation, and they are often seen as thematically complementary. </p><p>Influenced by the philosophers Søren Kierkegaard, Arthur Schopenhauer, and Friedrich Nietzsche, Camus argues that life is essentially meaningless, although humans continue to try to impose order on existence and to look for answers to unanswerable questions. Camus uses the Greek legend of Sisyphus, who is condemned by the gods for eternity to repeatedly roll a boulder up a hill only to have it roll down again once he got it to the top, as a metaphor for the individual’s persistent struggle against the essential absurdity of life. According to Camus, the first step an individual must take is to accept the fact of this absurdity. If, as for Sisyphus, suicide is not a possible response, the only alternative is to rebel by rejoicing in the act of rolling the boulder up the hill. Camus further argues that with the joyful acceptance of the struggle against defeat, the individual gains definition and identity.</p><cite>The Editors of Encyclopaedia Britannica</cite></blockquote>

<h2>up the hill we have gone, and down the hill we have fallen</h2>

<p>     684 days have passed since my last post. Throughout these almost two years, I have experienced, learned, thought, and sought, to extents that have been quite overwhelming. And though I have not been active on this site, my mind has been worked to exhaustion and back, providing me with immense joy as a result of my developments. </p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy.</p><cite>Albert Camus, The Myth of Sisyphus</cite></blockquote>

<p>     Though time may have passed, I will still readily quote Camus, as he has provided me with motivation to pursue the Sisyphean task of existence in spite of the absurd. These 684 days have felt like one of Sisyphus' trips to the top of the hill, and recently I've experienced the fall back down to the bottom of the hill in the form of a mental reset. The cogs that have been turning so feverishly have halted for some tuning and cooling down. And though initially it was quite disheartening, watching the boulder roll back down that frustrating, yet mystifying hill, at the end of it all "one must imagine Sisyphus happy." This was but one trip of many, and one of the most fruitful of trips it has been. It never fails to captivate me just how vast and immense the tasks of understanding our thoughts, emotions, rationales, and bodies are. And philosophically I feel that I have made just as much progress throughout this time as others when I was specifically studying philosophical works and topics. Of course that prior study was necessary in order for me to have such an experience whilst lacking similar philosophical study, but nonetheless it was a sort of personal chapter in philosophical development that I had never experienced. </p>

<p>     And now, back at the bottom of the hill, amidst the fog, I have paused. I've contemplated my next attempt to get that boulder up -- not up and over the hill, but back to the top. How do I want this attempt to play out? What method(s) do I want to use? What do I want to get out of this attempt? Should I even plan this out, or should I just start pushing? These questions have played out frantically in the mind, as if it was a pinball machine launching thoughts around to different ideas and perspectives. I will not be making this attempt blindly, or without a goal in mind, but instead with a sort of directed openness. I know how I want to start the trudge, but I'm open to adjusting on the way up. </p>

<p>     My content has been lacking -- or quite honestly, nonexistent. Expect more to come. Topics will vary, as I do begin graduate studies in the Cognitive Sciences next month. Dedicated philosophy/cognitive science posts will come, but so will some of my more traditional philosophy of life pieces. The fog is clearing once again, and I intend to make the most of the time before it returns, if ever.</p>
