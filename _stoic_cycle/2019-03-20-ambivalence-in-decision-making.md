<p>          Every day we face countless decisions. What time to wake up, what to eat for breakfast, whether or not to take time to eat lunch, what to wear, what to allocate our time for, etc. Some of these are easy, simple decisions to make. Others, are more difficult. Should I try and squeeze in some gym time between obligations and hope I'm not late? Should I go for it and ask for a raise today? What classes should I take next semester? These all require a little more time and deliberation, and sometimes some guts as well. </p>

<p>       If one has set guidelines for themselves, determining ways in which to act, behave, and believe, then many decisions theoretically become much easier to make. But even if not, there are many people who have a general set of moral guidelines they unknowingly follow that guide most of their decision making. These are the easy decisions. These are the ones where you know what's right or wrong, and you're simply deciding whether or not you're going to do what you believe is right or not. </p>

<p>     The difficult rises though, when we're faced with decisions that don't give us a clear right and wrong choice. Or where we know a decision is to be made, but we're not even sure what that decision is, or how to go about determining it. Personally, this is where I struggle the most, and where my ambivalence in decision making is most prevalent. Let's say, for example, that I'm in a situation in which I have a desired outcome in mind. But I have absolutely no idea how to bring about that desired outcome. What do I do then? Abandon deliberately seeking that desired outcome, air on the side of caution, and avoid any risk? Or do I go out of my way to either try and devise a way to formulate choices with roughly-predicted results, or just say screw it and risk it doing whatever comes to mind? These are usually the options at hand, and they're usually pretty hard to choose between. But what matters the most here, is making a choice. We're prone to over-analysis, and overthinking in these situations, but these can ultimately lead us to never making a decision at all. And this not only hinders progress, but takes from us an opportunity to learn and experience. If we're constantly debating on how to approach a situation, or how to approach our decision-making, but never making progress or settling on any decisions, then we're simply wasting our time. </p>

<p>     This relates back to my post on making use of our studies, in the sense that many philosophers stress the path of progress. Yes, the studying, learning, and development are crucial, but if one never takes those and applies them to move forward, then it was all a waste in the first place. I'm telling myself this repeatedly in my own every day life, and it has honestly helped. It has helped me transition from someone who would worry about the outcomes of decisions, which decisions to make, what if this, what if that, and would spend all my time thinking, but I never ended up <em>doing</em>. Now, although it may have been a bit drastic a transition, I try to embrace the essence of not only solidifying choices and decisions, but doing in general, and usually on the riskier side. Although this can have its detriments, so be it, this is life. Without risk, reward tends to be quite rare. And if struggling with ambivalence in decision making, risk will take one right out of that ambivalence, and into a whole new world. Something I will practice, and I hope others will as well, is occasionally, when faced with a difficult decision, to just say screw it and make a risky choice. See where it takes you, see where it doesn't, and use each of these experiences to soak in all that there is to learn from doing so. Because quite simply, there is so much more learned from a risky decision than a cautious or planned one. And this may be a possible solution to the issue of ambivalence in decision making. And although a somewhat initially uncomfortable one, like all other solutions in life, they start with one possessing, and directing their will power in such a way to progress forward, usually out of their comfort zone. Because more often than not, that is where progress lies. </p>
