<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Can the extent to which we use technology cause it to merge with our concept of self?</em></p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p class="has-text-align-center"><strong>Self: </strong>the union of elements that constitute the individuality and identity of a person</p>

<p class="has-text-align-center"><strong>Artificial Intelligence:</strong> the capability of a machine to imitate intelligent human behavior</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Imagine this. A blind woman is walking through a city plaza while using her white cane (walking stick). By use of this piece of technology, she is able to sense and feel the environment around her, allowing her to safely travel. Were it not for this tool, she may not even be able to find her way around! There some people who argue that due to this, this cane can be considered an extension of her body as her sensory system is directly incorporating its feedback into her cognition. Do you think this is the case? How about with someone who's deaf, but is able to regain their sense of hearing via an aid -- is this now an extension of their brain, body, or even their mind? </p>

<p>This is a portion of what one of the newer theories about the nature of cognition, our minds, and the brain, <strong>4E Cognition</strong>, touches on. As it gains traction and support in Cognitive Science it aims to support the claim that cognition is shaped and structured by dynamic interactions between the brain, body, and both the physical and social environments (Newen et al., 2018). These four "Es" are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>E</strong>mbodied: cognition involves the entire living system (brain and body)</li>

<li><strong>E</strong>mbedded: cognition displays layers of co-determination with physical, social, and cultural aspects of the world</li>

<li><strong>E</strong>xtended: the environmental and social resources that enhance the cognitive capacities of an agent are in fact <em>constituents</em> of a larger cognitive system</li>

<li><strong>E</strong>nactive: cognition arises through a dynamic interaction between an acting organism and its environment. This environment is brought about by the active exercise of that organism's sensorimotor processes.</li>
</ul>

<p>Now, what the heck does this have to do with our sense of self? Let me explain. What's interesting about this theory is that it calls into question our conception of our own cognition, and even our minds. Are my mind and my brain the same thing? Is my mind external from the body? Are they connected? Is my environment a part of my cognition and/or mind? These kinds of thoughts can call into question simple concepts that we may have always thought we never really had to debate. But, what if we come to think: </p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote has-text-align-center">
<p>Well, maybe my cane, or my environment, is a part of my cognition. </p>

<p>or... </p>

<p>Maybe my environment should be considered part of my mind since it actively shapes and influences it. </p>
</blockquote>

<p>Now we're going from cognition to, in a broader sense, evaluating part of what makes us, us -- and therefore questioning our sense of self. A sense of self is something imperative, integral, and innate to our existence as individuals, and as abstract a concept as it may be, it's one we seem to have a natural understanding of. The blind woman may not have all of these philosophical thoughts, but she may just unconsciously possess the belief that her cane is an extension of her self. </p>

<p>Okay, things are starting to come together... But why artificial intelligence? Take this example. 8am hits. Your smart home device that has been incrementally increasing light intensity for the past 30 minutes (to simulate a sunrise), wakes you up via a comforting alarm tone. "Okay, okay, I'm awake" you say. Alarm stops. The device then goes on to give you a look at your day. The weather, relevant news, traffic updates, your day's schedule, and a few other details are delivered to you. Your device then notifies you that one of your friends has sent you an invite to dinner at 7:30 tonight at the local Italian restaurant and asks if you'd like to accept. Just like that, it's on your calendar. It then finishes by automatically playing your favorite morning lo-fi playlist as you go about your morning routine. When you get around to making breakfast, you ask: "What should I have for breakfast?" The device conveniently provides you with a recipe for a breakfast burrito that it recommends based on your tastes in food lately. </p>

<p>Convenient, right? Sure! But the point here, is what have you had to actively do this morning? How much of your "cognitive load" was taken care of by your device? Not only that, but it even thought up the perfect breakfast for you! After weeks of this treatment passes, it feels like second nature. Your device knows you, it can think beyond your own capabilities at times, and its intelligent self even travels around with you via integration to other devices you keep close. It's almost like... it's become a part of you. You might even go so far as to say you've become one, and playfully joke that you "don't know how you'd live without it." These interactions often seem casual and minor on a day-to-day basis. But in the context we've established they seem particularly similar to embedded and extended cognitive processes. </p>

<p>All of this brings us back to the original question of whether or not technology can merge with our sense of self. Do you still feel the same way you did the first time you read that question? I went through a similar mental debate when I was confronted with the question: "What is the extent to which this extension of cognition is possible without confusing the concept of self?" The moment I was met with this question, my brain went into overdrive. Its easy to think of the walking cane or hearing aid being considered part of an individual's self, but from a philosophical perspective I hadn't really considered how a smart device, or even a smart car could be considered not just part of one's cognitive system, but even affecting their sense of self. </p>

<p>As technology continues on its path of rapid development we are seeing new applications arise day by day. More tasks that we no longer have to worry about, less thinking we have to waste our time on, and simply less things we have <em>to do</em>. But it's not that these things aren't happening or being done, it's that they're being done for us as a result of our own interaction with pieces of technology. If my technology is acting and producing in tandem with myself, and even influencing and shaping myself, its tempting to start to think that it may be justified to believe that it's somehow to be included in who I am. As the definition of self states, this tech may now be an element constituting my identity, directly and indirectly. </p>

<p>Whether its by protecting our privacy, embracing the functionality of artificial intelligence technology, or trying to walk the balanced path between physical independence and convenient/efficient reliance, I think its important that we all take the time to think about our interactions with technology, especially with how integrated its becoming with the world we live in. A few good questions we can ask ourselves are:</p>

<ul>
<li>What are some ways that I interact with technology? </li>

<li>To what extent do I use technology? </li>

<li>How does technology affect me (beneficially and/or detrimentally)?</li>

<li>How much of my day is spent interacting with technology?</li>

<li>How many tasks have I delegated or offloaded, onto technology?</li>

<li>Could I live a normal, functioning day, or even a week, without my usual technology? </li>
</ul>

<p>I think this topic and these questions are extremely important to consider as we continue living in an increasingly technological world, and the more effort we put into developing a thoughtful perspective on these matters, the better we'll coexist with technology. </p>
