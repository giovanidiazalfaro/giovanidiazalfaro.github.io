There are few things as satisfying and overwhelmingly pleasurable as the feeling you're left with after having a great conversation. We've all experienced it. You walk away after what may have been an extremely long or short conversation, and just think to yourself: "Wow, that was a great conversation. I wish I had those more often." But why is it that we wish so? Why do we hope for meaningful, enjoyable conversation as if it's something ever so rare and out of reach? Instead, why don't we try to improve the quality of our conversations?
<p style="text-align:center;"><em>Note: I will only be covering face-to-face conversations in this, purposefully choosing to not cover text message conversations.</em></p>

<blockquote>
<p style="text-align:center;">&nbsp; "In your conversation, don’t dwell at excessive length on your own deeds or adventures. Just because you enjoy recounting your exploits doesn’t mean that others derive the same pleasure from hearing about them." - Marcus Aurelius</p>

</blockquote>In my opinion, the greatest enemy of high-quality conversation is&nbsp;<strong>small talk</strong>. The hopefully short, almost meaningless friendly conversations we have with others on a daily basis. If one were to eliminate all the small talk of any single day, just imagine how much extra time they would have on their hands. Imagine just how little of a difference it would make to them not knowing how Nancy's day was, or how Jerry's garden project is coming along. Because in all honesty, do we really care about the details of the lives of all of these people?

This isn't to say that it's wrong to care, nor that we shouldn't. The goal here is to acknowledge just how much we simply don't care about, and therefore minimize the small talk. One of the slyest, yet largest occurrences of this takes place in greeting someone. A conversation might go like this for example:
<blockquote>
<p style="text-align:center;">*You enter your office and walk by Mark*</p>
<p style="text-align:center;">You: Hey Mark, how are you?</p>

</blockquote>Now when this interaction occurs, there are a few common scenarios that take place.
<ol>
 	<li>You continue to walk by Mark, he says "I'm doing pretty well" and continues to walk away. The conversation ends. He may even ask you how you are, and you keep walking without responding.</li>
 	<li>Mark doesn't answer and continues to walk by. You're unphased.</li>
 	<li>Mark responds by asking you how you are, as if he didn't even acknowledge that you had already asked him that; yet you both consider this to be a mutual greeting.</li>
 	<li>Mark stops, responds, and starts up a conversation of zero importance. You've now wasted fifteen minutes not even fully listening and blindly responding to pointless conversation.</li>
</ol>Sound familiar? It should. I know for example when I walk into work, this occurs on a daily basis like clockwork, and there are sometimes people who get offended if not greeted by you. Once again, this isn't to say that it is detrimental, as it can actually be pretty beneficial, giving those a sense of importance to others, and sometimes it quite literally can make someone's day. But the questions here are: Was any of this really necessary? Are we really making the most of conversation in these situations?

I recently watched a TED Talk in which one countered this phenomenon of "small talk" by introducing their idea of "big talk" in which they approached complete strangers, and instead of asking about their day, would ask them profound, and sometimes personal questions. The aim of this was to create more unique and strengthened connections with people by means of conversation that actually had purpose and substance. On the other hand, I watched another TED Talk in which one went over how to have a better conversation, and the absurdity of techniques intended to show someone that you're listening. Because quite honestly, if you're listening to someone, what need is there for you to prove that you're listening? Then you just end up not listening in an attempt to express signs that you are. Absurd, right?

In these cases it becomes quite obvious that to improve the quality of any given conversation, all one needs to do is aim to do so. Conversation shouldn't be meaningless, it is an activity, an exercise, and a skill. If one were to engage in any other activity, with a goal or purpose, they would do so with directed effort, right?&nbsp;What point is there in joining a track team, and then jogging your 100-meter sprint? All you've done is waste your time, and what little lack of effort you chose to exert. The same goes for conversations. They require energy, effort, and active participation. Talking to someone is not a simple, easy task. With active engagement, talking to someone for four or five minutes can yield more than many thirty-minute long talks, assuming they were four or five minutes filled with effort, purpose, and genuine inquisitiveness. Try skipping the common initial fifteen minutes of small talk that sometime end after those fifteen minutes, and have a conversation that matters. Ask something crazy. Ask something profound. Never met this person before? Cool. Start with: "Nice to meet you ____, I'm Giovani. I was just wondering, what makes you feel like you've had a meaningful day?" Now trust me, I know this sounds like the most ridiculous start to a conversation you've ever heard. But I can guarantee you, that so long as the other person or persons are just as willing to invest into a quality conversation, it will result in you walking away saying: "Wow, that was a great conversation." The only difference is, now you don't have to long for more of those. Now, you can strive to make each and every conversation into a "good conversation."

It doesn't necessarily always have to be such an extreme inquiry when initiating conversation. What matters, is that it is a meaningful inquiry or statement. Put effort into your thought, and don't blindly reply. Silence and pauses in conversation aren't bad. They usually mean you're actually thinking about what to say, or how to respond purposefully. And in that case, what more can we as the partner in conversation ask for? That's the whole point of this, isn't it? So from now on, if you're willing to do so, aim to partake in higher quality conversations. Don't rudely ignore all attempts at small talk right off the bat and condemn those for having engaged in such actions, because like I said, it's more a social practice than anything else, although it can be detrimental. But next time, maybe use small talk as a segue into "big talk." Next time, don't just settle for idle, blind conversation. Next time, try to have a good conversation.
