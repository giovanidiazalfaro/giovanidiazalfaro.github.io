<p style="text-align:center;"><strong class="mw_t_bc">core :&nbsp;</strong>a basic, essential, or enduring part (as of an individual, a class, or an entity)</p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><strong class="mw_t_bc">value :&nbsp;</strong>something (such as a principle or quality) intrinsically&nbsp;valuable&nbsp;or desirable</p>

<p style="text-align:center;">“At your core, who are you and who do you want to be? What is truly important to you? If you are going to do first things first — the main thing, is keeping the main thing, the main thing."                                                      - Ben Bergeron, on <em>Chasing Excellence</em></p>

<p>          Something that has been a priority of mine over the past year and a half, has been self-development. Not self-help, but self-development; as there are distinctions to be made between the two. I approach this self-development as a process that I acknowledge has no set endpoint, but nonetheless holds immeasurable value within its process. I am striving to pursue and cultivate the person that I want to be. To do this I need to have some goals in mind, and to set those goals I had to spend a lot of time asking myself questions.</p>

<p><em>&nbsp;          "What values do I want to uphold?" &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;</em></p>

<p><em>                                                                       "What's important to me?"&nbsp;</em></p>

<p><em>"Who do I want to be known as?" &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;</em> <br><em>&nbsp;                                                                         "Do I value virtue?"</em> <em> </em></p>

<p><em>&nbsp;                  "Who's opinion matters?"</em></p>

<p><em>&nbsp; &nbsp;"What has had an influence in creating the person I am today?"&nbsp;</em></p>

<p><em>                            "What do I believe to be morally and ethically good?" &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; </em></p>

<p>     Those as well as many more questions, are some that I explored in an effort to build a foundation off of which to pursue my aforementioned self-development. But of all the questions I pondered over, none gave me more difficulty than: "What values do I want to uphold?" I knew that I wanted to become a better person. I knew that I wanted to develop. I knew that I wanted to solidify a sense of self that I was content with. But what I couldn't figure out, were the guiding values that I wanted to be the basis for all of these goals. This isn't to say that I didn't know of any values I wanted to uphold, but more so that I struggled to pick those I wanted to identify as "core values."</p>

<p>     Most people who embark on any sort of self-developmental path do so to better themselves, or improve their well-being. And in this case, I would say it is safe to assume that most of those people want to be trustworthy, honest, calm, kind, relaxed, etc. There are myriad values that most would consider to be "good." But I felt that to truly get the most out of my own development, I had to stray away from such a broad approach. I felt that doing so would just be me pursuing being a good person, and being stuck with an unecessarily broad, and undefined goal. So, I told myself to choose four <strong>core values</strong>. This seemingly simplistic task ended up being one of the most difficult challenges I've ever faced. I continuously asked myself "Do I want to be _____?"; filling in that blank with every value I could possibly think of, and thinking to myself "Yes, I do want to be," for almost every one of them. Then I stopped myself. I took a second, and asked myself: </p>

<p style="text-align:center;">"If someone was to ask one of my friends to describe me, as a person, in three values, what would I want them to undoubtedly say as a response?"</p>

<p>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;First of all, going down to three did not make this any easier. But this is where progress began to be made. Almost instantaneously, certain values that were intermingled with all that I had been contemplating, seemed to shout out to me above the rest. There were clearly things that above else, I wanted to be.
</p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><strong>Honest</strong></p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><strong class="mw_t_bc">:&nbsp;</strong>marked by integrity</p>

<p>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;I want to be an honest person. Something I've always tried my absolute hardest to uphold, is to always either give an honest answer, or no answer at all. No matter what the question, I have told myself to never lie. In all honesty, so far upholding this rule has sucked. It tends to do more bad than good, and that constantly leads me to question whether or not this is something I want to continue to do. Would a white lie every now and then make situations better? I would ask myself. But I always came to the conclusion that upholding my honesty was the best course to take. Instead of looking at it from the perspective that I was giving answers to people that resulted in both positive and negative outcomes, I considered the perspective of the other person as well. If someone is willing to ask me a question, then they should be prepared to hear the answer, right? And if not, then they shouldn't be asking the question in the first place. After reaching this conclusion, I had no doubt left in honesty being one of my core values. Because I have no qualms with being known as a truly honest person, but also one who speaks their mind. And whether or not someone else is ready for the answer to a question they ask me is not of my concern anymore. If they're asking, they're getting my honest answer, regardless of the result. And that way, if they're not content with the answer, they themselves have learned a lesson as well.
</p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><strong>Collected </strong></p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><strong class="mw_t_bc">:&nbsp;</strong>possessed of calmness and composure often through concentrated effort</p>

<p>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;This one comes off as a bit obvious, with the title of this blog being The Stoic Cycle. But nonetheless, I above all else want to be a collected, stoic person. For a while now, I've been trying to minimize the effect of emotional stimuli on myself. I feel that being in control of one's emotions provides one with extreme control over one's mental faculties, which is invaluable to the process of self-development. This has also proven to be one of the most difficult values to cultivate. It doesn't simply mean staying calm when someone calls you something degrading, or not crying when your dog dies, or being an apathetic person. It means controlling your responses to stimuli. Whether it be those of sorrowful, angry, exciting, sexual, or other foundations. It means responding to each and every one of these stimuli in a cool, calm, collected, and above all else, logical manner. Becoming this kind of stoic being, not only gives you much greater control over yourself and your mind or makes you invaluable to anyone in any situation, but it also gives you the ability to deliberately, efficiently, and with dedicated effort, work to develop yourself and learn.
</p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><strong>Willing</strong></p>

<p style="text-align:center;"><strong class="mw_t_bc">:&nbsp;</strong>inclined or favorably disposed in mind</p>

<p>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;Although this is just as important to me as the formerly mentioned values, this was one of the hardest to find a dedicated term for. So consider to be a sense of willingness, as well as a sense of openness for the purposes of this post. This value is also interesting due to the cause for its having been chosen. Willingness is not a value that arose from my own deliberate contemplation. It is a value that I found so overwhelmingly attractive and genuine in another person that I myself chose to strive to embody it. The reason I used two terms to be used interchangeably as well, is because of my intended application of the value to my life. Not only do I aim to be someone who is willing to do almost anything, free of reluctance, doubt, or fear, but I also aim to be someone who is willing to listen or understand. I aim to be someone who is open to all. Including that which directly opposes anything I believe or hold dear. I chose to name this blog The Stoic Cycle as opposed to The Stoic Solarium, because the term cycle to me allowed for change. I am writing this blog to aid in my self-development and furthering of thought. And I am completely open to any change that may result from this path. For all I know, I might end up an existential fanatic, or a buddhist. But currently, I'm a pursuer of Stoic philosophy on a path of self-development, experiencing my own, unique cycle. I'm willing to experience, learn, and if need be reject new stimuli, ideas, and experiences. And I'm also just as open to these things as well.</p>

<p>     At the end of the day, I want to be known as an undeniably honest, collected, and willing human being. If there is ever any doubt as to my possession of any of these values, then it is clear that I have even more work to do. And at the end of the day, among all the values I aim to acquire, refine, and apply, these are my three core values. And these will be the motivations that guide my approach to life, and my self-development.</p>

<p style="text-align:center;">Side Note: Choosing and defining core values is a pretty fun, yet challenging exercise. So give it a shot if you’d like!</p>
