<p>This year we've had an influx of artificial intelligence (AI)-generated art taking the world by storm. From AI generating personalized avatars, to entire scientific articles, and going as far as to even create sexually-explicit images of adolescents and children, we have experienced an overload of diverse artistic creations generated by AI. This overload has led to many ethical concerns being raised regarding (but not limited to): the rights of artists being used in machine learning (ML) training databases, the role of AI-generated art in the competitive and market scenes, and the sociological factors at play in these systems. </p>

<p>The concerns over the rights of individual artists have had the spotlight on various social media platforms as of late. This is due to the way in which these AI-generators are trained. Essentially, they are exposed to ranges of thousands, to billions of art pieces usually with accompanying text descriptions. From these billions of image they then construct the predictive and generative capabilities we've seen. The rising concern, though, is that the artists of these billions of images are not having their influence(s) credited and/or compensated. This is definitely a complex topic, and a lot of social and legal discourse this year has emphasized how complex it is, as well as how much faster technology is developing compared to our regulation and understanding of it. To try and get a grasp of the issue(s), let's take a look at two approaches to training:<br></p>

<ul>
<li>General Training: By general training, I am referring to the systems that have been trained on extremely large numbers of art pieces. The case can most definitely be made here that the influence of any individual artist is practically negligible, as having their art removed from the database would have no noticeable effect on the art being produced. </li>

<li>Tailored Training: There have been a few cases this year where engineers have tailored a generative system to create in the style of a specific, current artist. This I feel intuitively raises red flags, as the database for training is usually primarily (if not fully) composed of the work of a specific artist, and therefore produces work extremely similar to theirs while maintaining an underlying and explicit systematic bias. </li>
</ul>

<p>I think it is clear that tailored training approaches infringe on the <em>intuitive </em>rights of artists. I express this point in this manner to emphasize that practically no work has produced definitive results on the questions of property rights with regard to artists and AI art systems -- with one artist in the US even receiving copyright for a graphic novel with images created using <em>Midjourney</em>. The murky ground here is when we discuss concepts such as "fair use" and the similarity of a creation to original works of an artist, or artists. Art has always been a realm where influence plays a core role. Gaugin was historically one of Van Gogh's greatest friends and influences, for example. And many artists train and hone their styles while studying the varied works of a wide number of artists with differing styles, mediums, and general backgrounds. The question is, where are we to draw the line between "artistic influence" and the infringement on the rights of an artist? Is it more of an accreditation issue, where we understand and accept the natural artistic process and the role influence plays, but take it more harshly when the intent is to mimic art and/or purposefully omit the presence of influence? If this is the case, then would it be helpful and/or anywhere near enough for these systems to have to maintain publicly-available cited lists of all influencing works and their creators? When you start to cross the line back into the general training systems it then calls into question whether applying these solutions to systems trained on millions, and potentially billions of works is possible. </p>

<p>One thing that we can't deny is when discussing the systems using the general training approach, originality is practically guaranteed. No two creations will ever be identical, and statistically an overwhelming majority of the art created has been unique and unrepresentative of anything existing prior. And honestly, a lot of these engines produce astoundingly beautiful work. Prior to writing this article I personally played around with a few of these engines quite a bit, and particularly with <em>Midjourney</em> I was shocked to see the intricacy and beauty of some of what I was able to create after working on a singular piece for a few hours. And these pieces, once again, were most definitely unique -- except when I didn't want them to be. One of the major points artists are making is that for more "notable" artists, their influence can be directly utilized via their naming in a prompt. Some might say that this isn't a big deal for those of the past such as Picasso, but what about modern day artists, and what's the difference? For example, I included the name of a phenomenal photographer Tim Walker in some prompts to see how this would play out (pictures below): </p>

<p>My piece (left; generated in <em>Midjourney</em>) is obviously an abstract piece, whereas Tim Walker (pictured right) is primarily a fashion photographer. Despite this, <em>Midjourney </em>is still able to put together art pieces utilizing his photographic style with quite a bit of accuracy and success. Initially the solution might seem simple -- censor the use of artists names in prompts. But realistically this is such an easy obstacle to work around that an effective solution would take a lot of effort by way of logic, and by way of establishing a level of artistic influence deemed deserving of censoring (once again screwing over the smaller artists). I am not attempting to provide a definitive solution in this article, but more so attempting to cover, educate on, and work through approaches to this very sensitive, complex, and unknown issue plaguing the art community, and on a larger scale AI and its relation to our global regulatory environment. </p>

<p>Applicable areas where this <em>seems </em>to be slightly easier to handle are the competitive and market areas. Just a few months ago an AI-generated art piece won 1st place in a Fine Arts competition, and competitors were furious. Although the work was unarguably a beautiful piece, it was generated using AI. At first glance this seems as simple as prohibiting entries generated by AI in these competitions and potentially starting competitions specifically for AI art -- or even labeling those pieces as "AI-Generated." But some of the AI-based artists have argued that in the same manner which these artists spend vast amounts of time crafting their entries, scrapping attempts, and in some competitions putting a lot of effort into post-processing, they are doing the same. Many AI-generated pieces are achieved after countless hours of work, similar to the time put into many other competition pieces. Not only this, but these artists sometimes take these images and then modify them in their own style of post-processing. From this view the generative process can come off as surprisingly similar. Ideas are had, attempts are made, influences are drawn, input is solicited (sometimes), and fine tuning/modifications take place. The question seems to be here, can we consider the AI-generators as "assistive tools" in the same manner as those used by the other artists? Similar points are to be referenced when placing these art pieces in a market scene, except many may see in the free market the methods of the artist don't need to be explicitly divulged in this way. My gut says no on the competitive front, but this is certainly a topic for further debate and discussion in both areas. </p>

<p>Sociologically these systems have definitely treaded on problematic grounds. One issue is regulating the contents of the art produced by these systems. There have been many cases where not only has a system produced explicitly nude art from a dataset that did not contain any nudity, but also produced sexually-explicitly content containing children and adolescents -- an extremely uncomfortable example being nude photos of one's younger self being generated from actual childhood photos. While many of these systems have prohibited gore and sexually-explicit content creation and input, there has (unsurprisingly) been a lot of content created with these features. And if the output is the only sexual content, is this because of the algorithms, or because users are inputting sexually-influenced content into the databases, transforming the algorithms? Can we hold them accountable, who are we holding accountable, and how do we frame the wrongdoing here? If it hasn't already begun, I don't think it will be long before sexual content is made with these systems and then marketed to the masses as a new digital product. This says a lot about the role of sexual content in our society, and also questions how we should be approaching it in this realm. </p>

<p>Tying this part of our discussion to a larger thread, we must acknowledge that by limiting these systems to influence via database, there is an indirect effect on the role of creativity and free will in creation (a topic I will more broadly cover in a following article). Biases are present in a lot of art, and limiting unique and creative input by implementing a foundationally-set database for influence only serves to limit the extent to which creativity and free will can produce these new art pieces. AI tends to glorify predictive capabilities, but the truth is that many believe we as humans possess some sort of free will (unless you believe in fatalism), and this is something that AI cannot account for. Human nature motivates us to overcome adversity and take on challenges. AI might predict a failure rate of 80% and suggest that a course of action be followed, but many of the most influential accomplishments in human history have taken place in that 20% success rate. This is where we cannot deny the power of an artist. Their creativity and will cannot be mimicked. Disney may succumb to replacing artists with AI systems, but the artistic skill of those like Miyazaki can never be replaced, or even close to matched by any system. And that is a major reason we appreciate him and Studio Ghibli, as well as their art. There will always be desire for the uniquely human creation. And although this is true, I do believe we need to further research and discuss these areas as I feel they disproportionately affect the early-career artists, and may even begin to sway those who considered starting art from even attempting the endeavor (as if it wasn't already hard enough). </p>
