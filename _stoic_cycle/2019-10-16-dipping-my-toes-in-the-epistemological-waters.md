<p>     To continue my trend of transparency, as well as to provide another source of reflection for myself in my development, I have decided to post my first attempt at a paper in the new and unknown subject to me of Epistemology. It has been a while since I felt somewhat uncomfortable writing on a topic due to the sheer queerness and anal tendencies of it. But nonetheless, here it is. </p>

<p class="has-text-align-center">Prompt: Assume that <em>S</em> believes that <em>p</em>, <em>p </em>is true, and this belief was produced by a cognitive process that reliably produces true beliefs. Yet, the believer does not know or justifiably believe that her belief that <em>p</em> was produced by a process that reliably produces true beliefs. She does not have any consciously-accessible evidence supporting her belief that <em>p</em>. <br>Does she know that <em>p</em>? </p>

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; In this paper I aim to present and
support my position that a person <em>S knows
</em>that <em>p </em>in a situation where we
assume that <em>S</em>&nbsp;believes that<em>&nbsp;p</em>,&nbsp;<em>p</em>&nbsp;is
true, and this belief was produced by a cognitive process that reliably
produces true beliefs.&nbsp;Yet the believer does not know or justifiably believe
that her belief that&nbsp;<em>p</em>&nbsp;was produced by a process that
reliably produces true beliefs.&nbsp;And, she does not have any
consciously-accessible evidence supporting her belief that&nbsp;<em>p. This
position brings up questions regarding a functioning principle that governs
what we consider to be known with respect to factors such as the reliability of
the process by which a true belief p</em>comes to be, the consideration of <em>causal</em> <em>standards </em>for said
belief <em>p</em>, and whether or not it
matters that <em>S </em>does or does not have
reason(s) to be aware of her belief <em>p</em>
being true. This is a topic particular to the matters of Historical and Genetic
theory and has been explored prior by Alvin Goldman and his theory of
Historical Reliabalism in his essay “<em>What
is justified belief?</em>” that I will be using as a supporting reference. </p>

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; A
major facet of my argument is that for <em>S</em>
to know that <em>p, </em>it is not a
requirement that she be aware of the cognitive process, or the reliability of
said cognitive process by which the true belief <em>p </em>came to be. What serves as grounds for her knowing that <em>p</em> in this case, is the cause of the
belief <em>p</em>. Since the cognitive process
which gave rise to said belief is one that is infallible, and consistently as
well as reliably produces undoubtedly true beliefs, I hold that she <em>can </em>know that <em>p</em>. This illustrates my reliance on the quality of causal standards
as a standard for judgement regarding what counts as knowledge. Concisely, I
hold that it can be known due to the reliability, truthfulness, and resulting
justification of said cognitive process. And as a reference for support as well
as refutation, I shall provide early attempts made by Goldman at a base-clause
principle for justified belief, although let it be known that at a certain
point further in the development of his theory, I do begin to disagree with him
on the matter at hand. </p>

<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Base-Clause
Principle (6<sub>A</sub>): “</em>If S’s
belief in <em>p</em> at <em>t</em> results (“immediately”) from a
belief-independent process that is (unconditionally) reliable, then S’s belief
in <em>p </em>at <em>t</em> is justified” (Goldman, 341).</p>

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; This clause, I agree
with for the most part. It is only after reviewing objections from others, as
well as developing my own differing views from Goldman’s further development
that I feel the need to alter it slightly. What I want to point out, though,
are a few nuances. One, I used A and not B deliberately. B in this case makes a
distinction necessary for conditional reliability, which we are not concerned
with. The unconditional reliability of this cognitive process is the greatest
strength of the argument for knowledge in this case. What we do not know in our
case though, are facts pertaining to the dimension of time, and to add, we are
also concerned with the matter of truth as a criterion. Given these
stipulations, I attempt to alter Goldman’s base-clause principle (6<sub>A</sub>):</p>

<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Base-Clause
Principle (α): </em>“If S’s belief in <em>p
</em>at <em>t<sub>x</sub> </em>is true, and results from a belief-independent
process that is infallibly, and unconditionally reliable at all times <em>t</em>,
then S’s belief in <em>p</em>is
justified, and it can then be concluded that S knows <em>p</em>.”</p>

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Given
this base-clause principle, we smooth out a few of the edges I felt were
troublesome prior. Now we have a principle that determines the warranting of
knowledge as a function of reliability of the causal process at all <em>t</em> to produce a belief <em>p</em> that is true. &nbsp;It is here though, that we face our first
objection. One, as Goldman proposes, might argue that “<em>some </em>justified beliefs do not derive their justificational status
from their causal ancestry” (Goldman, 342). For example, beliefs about
phenomenal states. If one is to use the common example of knowing that I am
“now” in pain, then our ideas hold their ground. This knowing of one’s own
being in pain gains its justification from the very short causal history
illustrated by the time passed from immediate sense perception to mental
apprehension and acknowledgement of the true fact. This is given that we can,
for the purpose of this argument, ignore philosophical arguments as to whether
or not sense perception can be considered a belief-independent reliable
process, which we will take it to be in this situation. Given that, S can know
that she is in pain <em>p</em>, given that the
conclusion was drawn from an infallible process at time <em>t </em>which also justified the belief. &nbsp;There are then objections tailored more so
toward Goldman’s following developments, and these happen to be where we split.
Goldman goes on to present:</p>

<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Base-Clause Principle (8)</em>: “If S’s
belief in <em>p</em> at <em>t</em> is caused by a reliable cognitive process, and S believes at <em>t</em> that his <em>p­</em>-belief is so caused, then S’s belief in <em>p</em> at <em>t</em> is justified
(Goldman, 344).</p>

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; My
main objection to (8) is the inclusion of the requirement that S believes at <em>t</em> that his/her <em>p</em>-belief is so caused. Whether or not S is aware of the cause of
their belief <em>p</em> has no influence on
the truthfulness, or justifiability of <em>p</em>.
Take for example, a situation in which there is an eight-year old child savant
who although has never studied sheet music or musical composition/harmony, can
replicate perfectly, any piece of classical piano composition perfectly on the
first attempt. This is a cognitive process that is infallibly reliable and
produces a true belief that <em>p</em> (the
child will replicate perfectly any said piece at <em>t</em>), yet completely unknown to the child. Because he doesn’t know of
or understand the means by which he can have the knowledge that he will
infallibly perform this task does not take away from its justifiability,
truthfulness, or ability to be considered knowledge. </p>

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Another
possible objection to base-clause principle (α), is the application of said
principle in “otherworldly” contexts. The case of the benevolent demon
presented in Goldman’s article is such a context. In this objection we have a
case where in another world <em>W</em>, wishful
thinking is considered a reliable cognitive process. In this world <em>W</em> exists a benevolent demon that
arranges things so “that beliefs formed by wishful thinking usually come true.
This would make wishful thinking a reliable process in that possible world”
(Goldman, 342). Now were we to take this as stated, it would be shot down by my
principle due to the fact that wishful thinking is not considered infallibly
reliable since the demon only “usually” makes these beliefs come true. But were
we to change it to “always,” we could then work with the situation as a
counterargument. In this case, wishful thinking turns out to be a reliable
cognitive process that results in true beliefs <em>p </em>at any time <em>t</em>. The
trickiness arises here, in the fact that there is deliberate manipulation on
the part of said demon taking place, validating the cognitive process. This is
still making it reliable, but at the cost of external manipulation. Given this
we can either adjust to validate any reliable process with respect to its world
<em>W</em>, or we can refute the validity of
deliberate manipulation. I do not like the idea of refuting deliberate
manipulation due to the fact that a hypothetical brain surgery could be done on
a person <em>S</em>, therefore unbeknownst to
them, constructing a reliable cognitive process such as the one mentioned
initially. This is deliberate manipulation, but I believe would not take away
from the knowledge resulting. So instead, I propose this:</p>

<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Base-Clause Principle (β):</em> “If S’s belief in <em>p </em>at <em>t<sub>x</sub>
</em>is true, and results from a belief-independent process that is infallibly,
and unconditionally reliable at all times <em>t</em> in its respective world <em>W</em>,
then S’s belief in <em>p</em>is
justified, and it can then be concluded that S knows <em>p</em> in world <em>W</em>.”</p>

<p>            With this principle (β) I rest my argument, standing by my position that what constitutes a true belief <em>p</em> as knowledge is based on the causal aspect of its coming into being, as well as the concept of infallible reliability at any time <em>t</em>, with respect to the residing world <em>W</em> of the individual <em>S</em>. By these arguments I hope to have shown the potential for using causal standards as a method of judgement, when in a situation where we are considering a cognitive process with exceptional reliability and resulting values of truthfulness. I do appreciate a few developments made by Goldman on the subject as well, though I feel my clause not only lends itself better to our situation, but also does a better job at addressing the concept of causality as a standard for judgement.  </p>
