<blockquote style="text-align:center;" class="wp-block-quote"><p>Without stirring abroad</p><p>One can know the whole world;</p><p>Without looking out of the window</p><p>One can see the way of heaven.</p><p>The further one goes</p><p>The less one knows. </p><p>Therefore the sage knows without having to stir,</p><p>Identifies without having to see, </p><p>Accomplishes without having to act.</p><cite>Lao Tzu, <em>Tao Te Ching, </em>"Book II Passage XLVII"</cite></blockquote>

<blockquote style="text-align:center;" class="wp-block-quote"><p>In the pursuit of learning one knows more every day; in the pursuit of the way one does less every day. One does less and less until one does nothing at all, and when one does nothing at all there is nothing that is undone.</p><p>It is always through not meddling that the empire is won. Should you meddle, then you are not equal to the task of winning the empire. </p><cite>Lao Tzu, <em>Tao Te Ching</em>, "Book II Passage XLVIII"</cite></blockquote>

<p>          Interestingly enough, these are two excerpts stemming from Taoist philosophy. And although Stoicism and Taoism have vastly different origins, there are cases in which their ideas and teachings seem to cross paths. These excerpt specifically, reference the Tao concept of <em>wu-wei</em>. This, simplified, resembles a lack of deliberate action, commonly expressed paradoxically as "action without action," or "action without intent." This idea comes off as contradicting most of what we are taught today, in which deliberate and directed action is the key to success and achievement. But in this case, Taoism stresses the importance that lies in simply going with the flow, or acting in accordance with nature, which is a key idea in Stoic philosophy as well. </p>

<p>     This idea though, is initially a difficult one for me to come to terms with. The thought that to accomplish much, I must refrain from doing much of anything at all, and refrain from attempting to influence or control any desired outcomes, is a tricky one. Stoic philosophy teaches acting in accordance with nature, using the pursuit of virtue as a guide. Simply do what is right, and use reason to do so. Accept what is out of your control, and leave it be. Accept what is in your control, and act accordingly. But on the other hand, we have Taoism almost implying that even acting on what we believe to be in our control is contradictory to the natural "way." They strive to live in an environment in which they accomplish "without having to act." The extent to which deliberate action seems to be the one matter slightly up for comparative debate, but nonetheless I believe both teachings hold value and knowledge within them.</p>

<p>     A practicing Stoic is taught to value virtue above all else. As mentioned prior, the acceptable norm is simply to do what is right, and to act in accordance with nature. This idea aims to develop a person into one whom seamlessly acts in a virtuous manner. The ideal state mentioned, is one in which decision making is not only rational, but also innate and always in accordance with beliefs and values. This ends up being very similar to the ideal Taoist, who practices "effortless action," simply participating in the natural flow of universal events, and in accordance with <em>Tao</em>, or "the way." </p>

<p>     What's valuable here, is the fact that two different schools of philosophical thought and practice, both strive for similar states of being regarding the manner in which to live and act naturally. So next time I feel the need to take action or am honestly worried about not influencing an event or its outcome, I am to consider these teachings, and both understand and accept that effortless action can be just as, if not more so beneficial than deliberate and directed action. And this also serves as a reminder that although I do focus primarily on the Stoic practices and teachings, there lies value in the thought and ideas of all other philosophical schools and belief systems as well, and to be constantly open to and accepting of these varying views and ideas is imperative to the development of the mind and self. </p>
